Another 'must' when in Florence is to walk over the Pont Vecchio, lined with gold and silver jewelry shops. In fact, it is not until you reach the middle of the bridge that you can see the river and realise that you are on a bridge. This photograph was actually taken from a window in the Galleria degli Uffizi (Uffizi Gallery). As a group we had a timed entry ticket to the gallery since the queues can be quite long. You are not allowed to take photographs of objects in the gallery but you can take photos looking out from the gallery.

There are two images that immediately show that you are in Florence; the cupola of the Duomo and the Statue of David. Michelangelo's David, a copy of which is shown here outside the Palazzo Vecchia, is now housed in the Accademia Gallery so as to protect it from the elements.

We left early for the one and a half hour journey from Chianciano to Firenze. We were dropped on the banks of the River Arno and walked through to the Piazza Santa Croce where we met our guide. The Basilica di Santa Croce (Basilica of the Holy Cross) is the principal Franciscan church in Florence and, as you can see below, has an impressive marble facade. According to our guide this is actually a recent addition (completed in 1863) and funded by Sir Francis Sloane, of Sloane's Lineament fame and after whom Sloane Square is named. It is odd that a Jew should pay for a new facade for a catholic church but it is thought that the star at the top is meant to form a link.

The Basilica was not open while we were there. Had it been, we could have seen the tombs of Michelangelo, Galileo, Machiavelli and Rossini.

The most photographed building however is probably the Cathedral of Florence, Basilica di Santa Maria del Fiore (Saint Mary of the Flower), otherwise known as the Duomo. Our guided tour ended here, amongst the crowds of visitors. Since Florence is so crowded, our guide used a radio microphone and we all had receivers and ear-pieces.

We could go inside the Duomo (but not take photographs) and decided to go up to the top of the cupola. However, the queue was very long, but there was no queue to go up the campanile (Giotto's Bell Tower).

You pay an entrance fee for the privilege of climbing the 414 steps to the top. Luckily, however, there are a number of levels and so you can stop for a rest at various points on your ascent, at each stage getting a more panoramic view.

As you might imagine, the view from the top is quite spectacular. In fact the view from the campanile is probably better than that from the cupola since you have a view of the dome. As a consequence, you take photographs from every corner, looking in every direction before starting the somewhat easier descent.

One of the buildings that you can see from the top of the campanile is the Palazzo Vecchio, which is the town hall of Florence and is on the edge of the Piazza della Signoria, said to be the political hub of the city. It is outside the Palazzo Vecchio that we find Michelangelo's statue of David (at least a copy of it), with many other bronze and marble statues around the L-shaped square.

We took the obligatory photos of each of us standing at the base of Michelangelo's statue, although David is decapitated in the photo with me acting the tourist! We also found that the wall surrounding the Loggia della Signoria (full of statues) was a good place to eat our lunch and people watch. Most days we would buy some form of filled bread or slice of pizza and find a suitable place to sit. Later we would buy two small tubs of ice cream, choosing from the display of flavours, which proved very difficult.

Opposite the facade of the Basilica di Santa Maria del Fiore is the Battistero di San Giovanni (Baptistery of St John), believed to be the oldest building in the city. It is an hexagonal, marble-clad building with magnificent gilded bronze doors on three sides. It took Ghiberti 21 years to complete the doors facing the Duomo. Michelangelo referred to these doors as fit to be the "Gates of Paradise" and the name has stuck, even though the original doors are now in a museum.

Originally the offices for the Florentine magistrates, the Uffizi gallery is a U-shaped building with long, wide corridors (lined with statues and paintings) and many rooms with mainly paintings of a religious theme. In fact there were very few that we recognised. Some that we did know were:

•The Birth of Venus by Botticelli

•Titian's Venus of Urbino

•Leonardo da Vinci's The Adoration of the Magi

•A self-portrait by Rembrant

•And some paintings of Venice by Canaletto.


After a long day we decided to eat in the hotel (not that there was much choice outside). It was a set menu of the usual four courses (anti-pasti, pasta, meat/fish course and desert) with a couple of choices of each. It was not as interesting as the previous day and, as we were to find when in Rome, not as good as you can get in a small, family-run restaurant.
