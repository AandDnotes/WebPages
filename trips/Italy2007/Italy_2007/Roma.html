Our diversion due to a fire on the way from Assisi to Rome meant that we were a little later arriving at the Hotel Oly than had been expected. The hotel was to the south west of Rome, only three stops on the metro from the Colosseum. The photo makes it look as though the hotel had extensive grounds. In fact it was only a few metres from the road. I took the photo through the fence of the office block opposite the hotel, so the grounds are part of the offices and there is a road on the other side of the hedge. Our room was on the top floor at the front. So we had a good view of the office block and the crane from the building site behind it!

Most of the group decided to eat in the hotel for that first night. It does not seem that anyone decided to eat there again. In fact, for our other two nights in Rome most of the party seemed to eat in a small family-run restaurant just around the corner. On the last evening we had a table outside, staying there until quite late.

 

Although there is a metro station at the Colosseum, we walked to the next station before catching the metro back to Basilica di San Paolo fuori le mura (Cathedral of Saint Paul outside the walls). This is one of the four papal basilicas, but, as its name suggests, is situated outside the walls of ancient Rome. Our hotel was only a ten minute walk from the station and another gelato kept us going!

That evening we returned to the same small restaurant and eat outside. In fact their tables on the pavement extended to spaces in front of neighbouring shops.

The next day was our departure from Rome but we had the morning (and early afternoon) before we left for the airport. So we took the metro into Rome again and explored a little more. We found pleasant gardens close to the Colosseum and walked through Circo Massimo (Circus Maximus) that is now just a large rectangular field but was the location of the notorious chariot races of Roman times. Whilst we were in Rome they were holding what was called 'White Nights' when entertainment was provided in some of the public places and many places stayed open into the early hours. Ciro Massimo was filled with hundreds of white globe lights, spread across the ground.

Our flight home was, luckily, uneventful and after all the walking we arrived home in need of a rest!

Our first morning in Rome started with a coach tour of the main tourist sites that could be seen from the road. A guide described what we were seeing and then we were left near one of the piazzas (Piazza Navona), from where some went on an organised walking tour. We decided to 'do our own thing' and set off in the direction of the Vatican City, with the intent of seeing most of the tourist sites in the northern part of the city that day. Consequently, our first photo of Rome is of St Peter's (Basilica di San Pietro). We found that the queue to go inside stretched over half way around the colonnade. We had been told that the queue for the Vatican Museum (in which you can see the Sistine Chapel) would also be very long at that time and so we decided to move on.

Mass was taking place in one area but visitors could walk elsewhere, apart from a central section of the floor. You could look up into the main dome but the aisles either side of the nave also had a number of small domes.

Going back outside, you could queue again (only for ten minutes) to go up to the top of the cupola. By paying a couple more Euro you could take the lift to the gallery that runs around the inside of the dome and then walk up the steps between the inner and outer skins of the dome to the top.

As you can see here, you get a very good view of the inside of the dome from the balcony. You also get a good view looking down towards the main alter.

The steps going up spiral their way around the dome, with the roof leaning more and more as you reach the top. Luckily there is one way up and another coming down, since the passage is quite narrow.

When you get to the top, the view is quite spectacular. You have an excellent view of the Vatican Gardens and of the Museum buildings, and a splendid view over the Piazza Pio XII down towards the River Tiber (Fiume Tevere).

Slightly left of centre in the photo above you can just make out the shape of Castel Sant' Angelo, which, in fact, is the mausoleum for Emperor Hadrian (of wall fame).

Here you can see Angela standing on the top of Castel Sant' Angelo, looking back at St Peter's. The mausoleum itself is seen from the middle of Ponte Sant' Angelo, the bridge of the Tiber.

The Pantheon is said to be the best preserved of all the Roman buildings. This seems to be due to the original temple being later used as a church. The portico is on the front of a circular building with a single opening (oculus) in the top of the dome (which is apparently made of concrete). It is nevertheless very light inside and amazing that you can walk on the marble floor that was trod by Romans.

At the end of our first full day in Rome we caught a bus from near the Monument to Victor Emanuel II to just around the corner from our hotel. The stop in Rome and the stop near the hotel were the two ends of the route, so we could not miss our stop. However, the many cobbled sections of road were not particularly comfortable! You can use the same tickets for the buses and the metro, validating it when you first use it. That evening we toured the area around the hotel looking for a restaurant, ending up in a small, family-run establishment that had been recommended by our tour guide. Quite a few members of our tour also eat there that evening. It was very good and some booked immediately for the following evening. We decided to take our chances.

The next morning we took the metro up to the Vatican Museum, with the thought of trying to get into the Sistine Chapel. Even early in the morning the queue seemed to stretch for miles and so, as mentioned earlier, we decided to go into St Peter's Basilica.

We then decided to walk through what appeared on the map to be an area of park, down to a particular bridge over the Tibre, crossing an island (Isola) in the middle of the river. However, we couldn't find the road we wanted and walked down a very inhospitable, busy road with no pavements. Eventually we came to the Fonte Acqua Paola, where a bride and groom were having their photographs taken. We saw three or four couples being photographed by landmarks in Rome.

Once over the river we walked through the Jewish Quarter (past another wedding) and on to the Forum.

It was hot by this time and some people took shelter in the shade of an olive tree amongst the remains of ancient Rome. Supplies of drinking water were never a problem. It seems that water from all the springs that issue from a variety of places around Rome is perfectly fit for drinking. So we simply refilled our bottles whenever they were getting low.

As you approach the eastern side of the Forum you see before you the mighty structure of the Colosseum. Apparently the design was such that it could be emptied of its 50,000 spectators within half an hour. Now, only one part of the perimeter wall is still standing (the part in the photo), with the rest of the elliptical building being surrounded by its interior walls. Again, queues and the heat prevented us from attempting to go inside.

Walking north along the banks of the river we came to the Piazza del Popolo and then the Borghese gardens, a good place to each lunch and have a rest. It is then a down-hill walk to the Spanish Steps, or more correctly, the steps leading down to the Piazza di Spangna, the location of the Spanish embassy.

From there it is a relatively short walk down a main road and then some narrow streets to the Trevi Fountain (Fontana di Trevi).

Here was probably the most crowded area that we came across during the whole of our visit to Rome. Perhaps it was just because it is a confined area with a large building in front of which is a semi-circular pool with ornate statues and a waterfall rather than a fountain. The fountain is actually the end of an aquaduct that brings water from a spring, 13 km away.

We stopped nearby for another galato before walking down the main shopping street in Rome (Via del Corso) and turning off to the Pantheon.

Before we left St Peter's we saw several members of the Papal Swiss Guard, which was founded in the 16th century.

We decided to return to St Peter's early the next day with the intent of trying to go up to the top of the cupola. We found the queue to be practically non-existent and were also surprised to find that you could take photographs inside the Basilica.
