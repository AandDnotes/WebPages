Lavada Walking

 

Most of the rain falls on the north of the island and on the central mountain chain. A few hundred years ago, land owners started building irrigation channels to bring water from the hills down to the southern slopes of the island. These channels, ranging from 15 cm to about one metre in width, are called ‘lavadas’. In the 1930s the government built many more and regulated the distribution of water. There are now over 2,000 km of lavadas on Madeira.


Since the lavadas need to be cleaned out each year there is a path by the side of each, which are very suitable for walking.

It was cold and windy when we went on a 12 km walk alongside two lavadas at a height of about 1,400 m. We went on an organised walk, with a local guide.
